A Practical Tactile Exploration with Enhanced Sampling for Fast Shape Estimation

Fig. 1. The tactile exploration process is used for object shape estimation.

Abstract: Automated measurement and three-dimensional reconstruction help robots to understand complex environments. In situations of darkness or smoke, tactile perception is pivotal. Here, we propose a tactile exploration method to estimate the shape of objects with Gaussian process implicit surfaces (GPIS), especially considering the sparsity and unevenness nature of tactile data. A data enhancement method is employed to improve the data integrity of tactile exploration for the implicit surface construction, which combines the external and internal position information of the object. In addition, a practical sampling strategy is constructed to improve the reconstruction efficiency limited by the nonuniform distribution of tactile data. This strategy considers the local data sparsity, shape uncertainty, local shape curvature, and exploration path cost for systematic and comprehensive data collection. Simulation and experiment results show that our method improves the model accuracy by 35.03% in the Hausdorff distance with the same sampling steps compared to other methods.

Introduction
Automated measurement and three-dimensional (3D) reconstruction are key for robots to perform operational tasks. Robots can use different sensors to acquire information about objects or the environment [1],[2]. Vision cameras can often provide robots with rich sensing data [3],[4]. However, in certain environments, such as darkness or smoke, the perception capabilities of vision cameras may be limited. More specifically, the tactile perception is attractive in special scenarios such as oil well scenarios [5] and rescue scene after fire. In these cases, robots can sense their surroundings through tactile sensors, which provide accurate information about objects through physical contact [6]. Our paper focuses on how to achieve shape reconstruction of objects using tactile perception. Manipulation tasks that relate to three-dimensional (3D) shape estimation using tactile sensors are often called tactile exploration.

Vedio for Real World Experiments

Conclusion: In this work, we propose a tactile exploration method to quickly accomplish 3D reconstruction of objects without visual or laser sensors. This method consists of two main parts: 1) a data augmentation method suitable for tactile exploration that can effectively deal with the sparsity of tactile data; and 2) a comprehensive active exploration strategy that adapts to the situation of sparse and unevenly distributed data at the beginning of exploration. We validated the proposed method through experiments and simulations. Compared to other methods, our method improves the model accuracy by 35.03% for HD with the same sampling steps. We also applied the proposed method to object recognition. We could get 80% accuracy after 10 explorations.
In practical applications, a robot would first collect several initial pieces of data about the object and determine the exploration area by performing random touches. Then, it can complete the modeling of the object based on the proposed method. Finally, we will get an acceptable shape estimate. This process provides a cognitive foundation for the subsequent manipulation of objects by the robot. The proposed method is not limited to the GelSight sensor used in our experiments. In the next step, we will investigate robotic object grasping operations based on reconstruction results.
The GelSight used for the experiments already has strong tactile sensing capabilities. However, due to its size, it can be limited for touch on complex objects, especially locations where space is tight, such as the back of a handle on a mug. In the future, we plan to conduct experiments using sensors that are small and easy to mount on robots.
